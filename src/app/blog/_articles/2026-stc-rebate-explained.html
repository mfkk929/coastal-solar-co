The STC (Small-scale Technology Certificate) scheme is Australia's main residential solar rebate. In 2026 it's worth approximately $2,800 to $3,400 on a typical 6.6kW system installed in NSW, and $4,200 to $5,200 on a 10kW system. You never handle the paperwork — your installer applies the rebate as an upfront discount on your invoice, then sells the certificates themselves. The critical detail most homeowners miss: the scheme steps down every year on 1 January, and ends entirely in 2030. A system installed today generates more certificates than the same system installed next year. Here's how it actually works, what you actually receive, and how to tell a legitimate rebate line-item from a number an installer has made up on the spot.

Key fact: A 6.6kW solar system installed in Wollongong in 2026 generates approximately 82–88 STCs. At the typical 2026 clearing-house price of $34–$38 per certificate, that's a point-of-sale discount worth $2,800–$3,400.

What Is the STC Scheme?

STCs — Small-scale Technology Certificates — are tradeable certificates issued by the Australian federal government under the Renewable Energy Target (RET) legislation. Every eligible solar, solar hot water or small wind system installed in Australia generates a specific number of STCs based on the system's capacity, its expected output over its remaining deeming period, and the climate zone where it's installed. Each STC represents one megawatt-hour (1 MWh) of clean energy expected to be generated (or displaced, in the case of solar hot water) over the system's deeming period.

These certificates are then sold in an open market. Larger energy retailers and liable entities are legally required to surrender a certain number of STCs each year to meet their renewable energy obligations — so they buy certificates from the open market or through the government's clearing house. The cash your installer receives for selling your system's STCs is what funds the upfront discount you see on your quote.

In other words: the STC rebate is not a government cheque. It's a market mechanism the government created, where your system earns tradeable certificates, and your installer monetises them on your behalf and passes the value back as a point-of-sale discount.

How Many STCs Does Your System Generate?

Three variables determine how many STCs your specific solar system earns:

  1. System capacity (in kW). A 6.6kW system generates more STCs than a 3kW system, obviously.
  2. Your climate zone. Australia is divided into four zones (Zone 1 = sunniest, Zone 4 = least sunny). All of NSW coastal areas — including Wollongong, Sydney, the Illawarra, South Coast, Newcastle and the Central Coast — sit in Zone 3. Inland NSW and most of Victoria are Zone 4; most of Queensland and WA are Zones 1–2.
  3. The remaining deeming period. Up until 2030, systems receive a deemed number of years of future generation. That deeming period shrinks each year the closer we get to the scheme's end.

The formula is roughly:

STCs = System size in kW × Zone rating × Remaining deeming years × 0.001 (rounding rules apply)

For Zone 3 (which includes Wollongong, Kiama, Nowra, Sydney and the Illawarra), the zone rating is currently 1.382 MWh/kW/year. Putting real numbers in:

  • 6.6kW system installed in Zone 3 (Wollongong) in 2026: approximately 82–88 STCs
  • 10kW system in Zone 3 in 2026: approximately 124–133 STCs
  • 13kW system in Zone 3 in 2026: approximately 161–173 STCs

The exact number is calculated by the Clean Energy Regulator from the date your system is commissioned. You don't calculate this yourself — your installer does it, submits it to the regulator, and the certificates are created in the REC Registry in your name before being transferred to whoever's buying them (usually your installer or an STC trader they work with).

What Is Each STC Worth?

STCs are a traded asset, and the price moves. Two markets exist:

  • Open market (spot price). Typically ranges $35–$39 in recent years. Depends on supply-demand balance between installs happening around Australia and the mandatory STC surrender requirements on energy retailers.
  • Clearing house (fixed price). The government operates an STC Clearing House at a capped price of $40 per certificate. Sellers can list STCs here and they'll eventually be purchased — but there's a queue, so proceeds can take months.

Most installers sell directly to STC traders or agents in the open market at the prevailing spot price, which runs a couple of dollars below the $40 clearing-house cap. In 2026, the effective per-STC value flowing to your rebate has typically been $34 to $38. That's the number your installer uses internally when calculating your upfront discount.

So for a 6.6kW Wollongong system with 85 STCs, at $36 per STC: 85 × $36 = $3,060 off the sticker price.

Key fact: The STC price is the only part of the rebate that's variable. Your system size and zone fix the number of STCs; the market determines their dollar value. Good installers lock in a price at the time of quote and absorb small market fluctuations themselves.

How the Rebate Is Applied at Point of Sale

The practical customer experience is simple. You receive a quote that already has the STC rebate deducted. It typically looks like:

  • Gross system price (before rebate): e.g. $8,800
  • Less STC rebate (e.g. 85 STCs × $36 = $3,060): -$3,060
  • Net price you pay: $5,740

You pay the net price. You don't see the $3,060 cheque. The installer takes ownership of your STCs (you sign over the right to create them), submits the creation paperwork to the Clean Energy Regulator after your system is commissioned, and sells the certificates themselves.

This system exists because homeowners previously had to register in the REC Registry themselves, create STCs manually, and find buyers. It was a nightmare. Since 2011, almost all installers have offered point-of-sale assignment, which takes the paperwork out of your life.

One subtle thing to check on your quote: that the rebate line item is explicitly shown. Cheap operators sometimes bundle it into the "total" number so you have no way of verifying whether the STC rebate was fairly applied or whether they pocketed part of it. A fair quote will always list the STC count, the per-certificate price used, and the total rebate value separately.

The Annual Step-Down and 2030 End Date

The STC scheme is on a scheduled phase-out. Every year on 1 January, the deeming period drops by one year. This means a system installed in January 2027 earns fewer STCs than the same system installed in December 2026. By 2030 the deeming period reaches zero and the scheme closes.

In practice, here's what that step-down looks like for a 6.6kW NSW system:

  • 2026: ~85 STCs → ~$3,060 rebate
  • 2027: ~75 STCs → ~$2,700 rebate
  • 2028: ~65 STCs → ~$2,340 rebate
  • 2029: ~55 STCs → ~$1,980 rebate
  • 2030: Scheme ends. No STC rebate available.

That's roughly $350–$400 less rebate for every year you wait. For the $8,800 gross system we priced earlier, waiting from 2026 to 2027 means paying closer to $6,100 instead of $5,740 — an extra $360 out of pocket for the same system.

What a Legitimate STC Line Item Looks Like

Several sales tactics abuse customer confusion about the STC rebate. Watch for these:

  • Inflated STC pricing. A quote that claims $40 per STC is using the clearing-house cap. That value isn't what your installer will actually receive (the clearing house has a months-long queue). Legitimate 2026 pricing is $34–$38.
  • Exaggerated STC counts. Some quotes round generously. A 6.6kW system in NSW Zone 3 in 2026 generates 82–88 STCs. If a quote claims 95 or 100, they're either using future-dated pricing rules or padding numbers.
  • "Free solar" or "$0 upfront" marketing. These deals almost always involve finance at above-market rates, with the STC rebate being used to paper over a loan that leaves you paying more than an honest cash purchase. The STC rebate is worth the same amount whether you finance or pay cash — it shouldn't create magically free systems.
  • Separate "government rebate" and "STC rebate" line items. There is no separate federal solar rebate. If a quote shows two different federal rebates, someone is either confused or deceiving you.
  • Price increases after quote acceptance. Your installer has control over when they submit the STC creation paperwork. If they delay creation into a new year, they pocket the extra certificates' value. A reputable installer locks STC pricing at the quote date.

STC vs Other Rebates — Don't Confuse Them

A lot of NSW homeowners confuse the STC rebate with state-specific battery rebates and historical solar rebates that have ended. Here's the 2026 map, cleanly:

  • STC rebate (federal, current): Applied to the solar panel system itself. Worth $2,800–$3,400 on a 6.6kW in Wollongong. You do not apply for it — your installer handles it.
  • NSW Cheaper Home Batteries Program (state, current): Applied to battery storage specifically, not the solar panels. Worth up to $2,000 off a 10kWh battery. Income-tested eligibility.
  • Federal Battery Rebate (federal, current from 2025): Additional rebate for battery storage introduced in the 2025 federal budget. Stacks with the state scheme.
  • NSW Solar Bonus Scheme (state, CLOSED): This was the old 60¢/kWh feed-in tariff scheme that ran 2009–2011 and ended in 2016. If someone references this in a 2026 quote, they're either deeply out of date or misleading you.
  • State feed-in tariff (state, current): Not a rebate — this is the 5–8¢/kWh you get paid for electricity you export to the grid. Ongoing, not a one-time incentive.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much is the STC rebate worth in NSW in 2026?

For a 6.6kW system in NSW (Zone 3 — which covers Wollongong, Sydney, the Illawarra, South Coast, Newcastle and the Central Coast), the STC rebate is worth approximately $2,800 to $3,400 in 2026. For a 10kW system, the rebate ranges $4,200 to $5,200. The exact amount depends on the per-certificate market price, which floats between about $34 and $38 per STC.

Do I have to apply for the STC rebate myself?

No. Since the late 2000s, almost all Australian solar installers offer point-of-sale STC assignment. You sign over the right to create your system's STCs when you sign your contract. Your installer handles the paperwork with the Clean Energy Regulator after your system is commissioned and deducts the equivalent rebate value from your invoice upfront.

Is the STC rebate the same across all Australian states?

Not quite. The rules and dollar value per STC are identical nationally — the STC scheme is a federal program. But the number of STCs your system generates depends on your climate zone. Sunnier zones (Zone 1 in northern Queensland and WA) earn more STCs per kW installed; less sunny zones (Zone 4 inland NSW and southern states) earn fewer. NSW coastal regions, including Wollongong and the Illawarra, are Zone 3.

Can I miss out on the STC rebate if I install too late in the year?

No — but you can miss out if you delay into a new calendar year, because the deeming period drops on 1 January each year. A system installed on 20 December 2026 generates more STCs than an identical system installed on 5 January 2027. The rebate value difference is typically $350–$400 for a 6.6kW system, which is a meaningful amount if you're on the fence about timing.

Does the STC rebate apply to battery storage?

No. STCs are generated for solar panels, solar hot water and small-wind systems — not for battery storage. Battery storage in NSW is supported through a separate program: the NSW Cheaper Home Batteries Program, stacked with the federal battery rebate introduced in 2025. The two schemes are commonly combined at point of sale by CEC-accredited installers. See our Cheaper Home Batteries Program explainer.

What happens after 2030 when the STC scheme ends?

The STC scheme closes at the end of 2030. From 2031 onwards, residential solar will no longer receive this federal rebate mechanism. Whether a replacement scheme will be introduced is unclear as of early 2026. Homeowners considering solar should factor this end-date into their timing: the rebate drops each year and disappears entirely from 2031. That said, solar economics remain strong even without the rebate — it simply extends payback periods by 12–18 months on a typical Wollongong install.

Is it better to wait for panel prices to drop, or install now with the bigger rebate?

Panel prices have been roughly flat for 3 years after falling steadily through the 2010s. The rebate, on the other hand, drops meaningfully each year. For the foreseeable future, the combination of stable hardware prices and declining rebates means installing sooner almost always beats waiting. Every month of delay costs you more in missed electricity savings than you save in hypothetical price drops.
